Lady Bird Deeds, Trusts & MERP: The Texas Homeowner’s Protection Plan

The Lady Bird Deed — also called an Enhanced Life Estate Deed — is one of Texas's most powerful and underused estate planning tools. Includes a real story, complete strategy comparison table, and timing guide.

Opening Story: “A House That Waited Too Long”

When Mr. and Mrs. Alvarez bought their little brick home in Houston in the 1980s, they assumed that when they passed, their daughter Sofia would simply “get the house.”

But after Mrs. Alvarez’s death, the home got stuck in probate for eight months. Sofia paid the taxes and upkeep, but she couldn’t refinance, sell, or even legally live there. Later, Medicaid sent a letter demanding reimbursement for her mother’s nursing-home care under MERP — the Medicaid Estate Recovery Program.

By the time everything was settled, Sofia spent over $12,000 in legal fees just to inherit the home her parents thought they had already secured for her.

This story isn’t rare — it’s almost the rule.

But it doesn’t have to be.

When Texans think about protecting their home, they usually imagine insurance policies, good maintenance, and solid title work. But there’s one threat many don’t see coming — the Texas Medicaid Estate Recovery Program, or MERP.

MERP is one of those programs most people never hear about until it’s too late. It’s designed to recover costs the state paid for a person’s Medicaid long-term care after they pass away — often by placing a claim against the person’s estate. And yes, that includes their home.

But here’s the good news: with the right planning tools — like Lady Bird deeds or certain trust structures — you can protect your property from MERP and help your heirs avoid the probate process altogether.

What Exactly Is MERP?

The Texas Medicaid Estate Recovery Program allows the state to recoup some or all Medicaid expenses after a recipient’s death by filing a claim against their estate.

This usually applies when:

  • The deceased was 55 or older when they received Medicaid benefits, and
  • They received long-term care services such as nursing home care or home-based assistance.

If the home is part of their probate estate, Texas can file a claim or place a lien against the home to recover costs but only after their death— potentially forcing the sale of the property to pay back the state.

That means even if you’ve worked your whole life to pay off your home, it could be taken after death to settle these costs if you didn’t set up the right protections in advance.

Here’s the catch:

  • The house remains safe while they’re alive (Medicaid cannot force a sale).
  • But once the homeowner dies, the state can attempt recovery — unless the property transfers outside of probate.

That’s the loophole.

MERP only attaches to assets that go through probate.

NOTE: Some states have a similar law in place so check your own home state laws to see how the rules surrounding Medicaid may affect your estate.

The Escape Hatch: Non-Probate Transfers

The way to sidestep both probate and MERP is simple in concept:

Move property title in a way that bypasses the probate court entirely.

There are several tools that can accomplish this — but the two most powerful (and Texas-specific) are the Lady Bird Deed and the Transfer-on-Death Deed.

Enter the Lady Bird Deed (Enhanced Life Estate Deed)

A Lady Bird Deed, also called an Enhanced Life Estate Deed, is one of the most powerful tools Texans have to keep their property out of probate and protect it from MERP.

Here’s how it works:

  • You (the homeowner) keep full control of your property during your lifetime.
  • You can sell, refinance, or change your mind at any time — no permission needed from your beneficiaries.
  • When you pass away, the property automatically transfers to your named beneficiaries outside of probate.

Because the property passes outside your probate estate, it’s generally not subject to MERP recovery.

Key Benefits

Avoids probate — no lengthy court process for heirs.

Bypasses MERP — property usually isn’t part of the recoverable estate.

Retains control — you keep full ownership rights while alive.

Simple and affordable — no need for a full-blown trust in many cases.

Potential Drawbacks

Limited to Texas and a few other states — this is a state-specific strategy. Check your own state’s laws to see if there is a similar tool to a Lady Bird Deed.

Still must meet Medicaid exemptions — certain transfers could cause penalties if done too close to applying for benefits.

Needs careful drafting — must use correct “enhanced life estate” language to ensure protection.

Trust Options That Protect Against MERP

For more complex estates or when multiple properties or assets are involved, a revocable or irrevocable trust can offer additional protection and flexibility.

  • Revocable Living Trust: Keeps assets out of probate but does not fully shield them from MERP because they can still be counted as part of your estate.
  • Irrevocable Trust: Offers stronger protection if structured properly because the property is legally no longer “yours.” However, this requires giving up control and must be established well before applying for Medicaid.

Pro Tip: Always work with an estate planning attorney familiar with Texas Medicaid law — not all trusts are created equal, and a misstep can undo your protection.

What Happens If You Don’t Plan Ahead

Without a Lady Bird Deed or trust in place:

  • The property passes through probate.
  • MERP can file a claim against your estate.
  • Heirs may have to sell the home or pay the claim to keep it.

In short: you could lose the legacy you spent a lifetime building — all because of a preventable paperwork issue.

What Probate Really Is (and Why It’s a Problem)

Probate is the court-supervised process that verifies a will, appoints an executor, pays off debts, and distributes property.

It sounds tidy — but in real life, it’s expensive, slow, and very, excruciatingly public at a time when all the bereaved want to do is grieve privately.

⏱ Timeline: 6–18 months (often longer in large counties)

Cost: 3–8% of the estate value in court costs and attorney fees

Visibility: Everything filed in public record — including who inherits what

Complications: Frozen titles, insurance gaps, property taxes missed, or even foreclosures while waiting

For families who simply want to keep the home in the bloodline or sell quickly, probate can feel like being handcuffed by red tape.

Smart Strategy for Homeowners and Heirs

If you or a family member may ever need long-term care or Medicaid support, start planning now.

Here’s what to do:

  • Consult a Texas estate planning or elder law attorney.
  • Ask about the Lady Bird Deed — or whether an irrevocable trust fits better for your situation.
  • File the deed with your county clerk so it’s legally recorded.
  • Keep clear documentation of your intent and beneficiaries.

With these steps, your home transfers seamlessly to your loved ones — no probate, no MERP claims, no unnecessary loss.

Bottom Line

For Texans, the Lady Bird Deed is one of the simplest, most powerful estate planning tools available. When paired with sound trust strategy and legal guidance, it allows you to:

Protect your home from state recovery,

Avoid probate court delays,

Keep your legacy intact for your loved ones.

Because in the end, wealth isn’t just about what you build — it’s about what you get to keep and who you pass it on to.

Understanding the 5-Year Look-Back Rule — and How to Legally Avoid MERP with the Right Estate Tools

When it comes to protecting your home and legacy, timing is everything — especially if long-term care or Medicaid benefits could ever come into play. This is where the 5-Year Look-Back Rule enters the picture.

What Is the 5-Year Look-Back Rule?

When someone applies for Medicaid long-term care benefits, the government reviews all financial transactions made by the applicant within the five years (60 months) before their application date.

If the applicant gave away property, transferred ownership, or sold assets for less than fair market value during that five-year period, Medicaid assumes those actions were done to qualify for benefits — and imposes a penalty period.

During that penalty period, Medicaid will not pay for nursing home care, meaning the individual must pay out-of-pocket until the penalty ends.

⏰ When Does the Clock Start?

The look-back period begins the day you apply for Medicaid, not the day you enter a nursing facility.

So, if you transfer your home or assets within five years before applying, those transfers could be scrutinized.

Example:

If a homeowner gifts their home to their children in 2023 and applies for Medicaid in 2025, the transfer falls within the five-year look-back and could result in a penalty.

How Lady Bird Deeds Protect the Home

A Lady Bird Deed (also known as an Enhanced Life Estate Deed) is a Texas-specific tool that lets you retain full control and ownership of your home during your lifetime, but automatically transfer it to your chosen beneficiaries upon death — without going through probate.

Power Move:

The Lady Bird deed is one of the only legal instruments in Texas that can protect a home from MERP without giving up control or ownership rights while alive.

Here’s the key:

Because you retain control (you can sell, refinance, or revoke the deed anytime), the property is not considered a completed gift during your lifetime.

That means:

  • ✅ It does NOT trigger the 5-year look-back rule.
  • ✅ It shields the home from Medicaid Estate Recovery (MERP) after your passing.
  • ✅ It avoids probate, saving heirs time and legal expense.
  • ✅ It’s revocable, so you’re never locked in if your plans change, allowing you to stay in control.
  • ✅ Avoids gift penalties since you’re not gifting your house away early causing a tax to be imposed on the recipient.

Using Trusts Strategically

If you live in a state that doesn’t recognize Lady Bird Deeds (like California, New York, or Illinois), a trust — particularly an irrevocable trust — can achieve similar protection when set up five years or more before applying for Medicaid.

  • An Irrevocable Medicaid Asset Protection Trust (MAPT) can hold your home and other assets outside your personal estate.
  • You give up ownership but retain the right to live in the home and receive income from trust assets.
  • After the five-year mark, those assets are shielded from Medicaid recovery.

Note: A revocable living trust does not protect assets from Medicaid recovery because you still legally own and control them.

⏳Timing It Right

Here’s the general timeline rule of thumb:

When to Seek Help

Estate and Medicaid planning should ideally happen before a health crisis.

If your parents or elderly clients are still healthy, now is the best time to:

  • Consult an Elder Law Attorney or Certified Medicaid Planner
  • Evaluate whether a Lady Bird Deed or Trust fits their goals
  • Confirm how your state treats Medicaid recovery and look-back rules

‍♀️ Pro Tip:

If your client (or you) are a Texas homeowner, filing a Lady Bird Deed today can potentially save heirs tens of thousands in probate and MERP claims later — with zero loss of control during your lifetime.

If you’re in a different state, check if Transfer on Death Deeds (TODDs) or Medicaid-compliant trusts provide the same function. The earlier these are established, the stronger the protection.

Transfer-on-Death Deeds (TODD): The Simplest Way to Skip Probate in Texas

Most homeowners assume their property automatically passes to family when they die. It doesn't. The TODD lets you designate beneficiaries who inherit instantly, bypassing probate entirely — with a full Who Benefits Most comparison table.

Most homeowners think that when they die, their home automatically goes to their spouse or children. Unfortunately, that’s rarely true — and probate courts make sure of it. Probate is slow, costly, and public. But there’s a little-known tool that lets you bypass all of it while keeping control of your property until the day you die: the Transfer-on-Death Deed (TODD).

For homeowners seeking a straightforward way to pass property to heirs, the Transfer-on-Death Deed (TODD) is a powerful tool. Unlike more complex instruments like Lady Bird deeds or trusts, TODDs allow property to transfer directly to named beneficiaries upon death, avoiding probate entirely.

What Is a TODD?

A TODD is a revocable deed that names one or more beneficiaries who will automatically inherit the property upon the owner’s death.

Key features:

  • Avoids probate: Property passes directly to beneficiaries.
  • Revocable: Owner retains full control during their lifetime.
  • Simple to execute: Typically requires recording with the county deed records.
  • Limited protection: Does not shield against Medicaid recovery or creditor claims like a Lady Bird deed.

It functions much like a “Pay on Death” designation on a bank account, but for real estate.

Once recorded, the deed sits quietly on file until the owner passes away. At that moment, ownership transfers directly to the named beneficiary. The home never becomes part of the deceased’s probate estate.

Why They Work

  • Simplicity: Quick and cost-effective compared to trusts.
  • Control: Owner retains full rights — to sell, refinance, or revoke the deed anytime.
  • Immediate Transfer: Heirs take ownership automatically at death, bypassing the probate process.
  • Accessibility: Available in many states, making it a flexible tool for estate planning.

Who Benefits Most

ScenarioBenefit
Homeowners with straightforward estatesAvoids probate without complex legal structures
Families wanting clarity for heirsNamed beneficiaries inherit automatically
Clients seeking simplicityLess expensive and easier to manage than a trust
Individuals concerned about speedProperty transfers immediately after death

Conditions and Requirements

  • Must comply with state-specific rules regarding TODDs.
  • Beneficiaries must be clearly identified in the deed.
  • Deed must be recorded with the county to be valid.
  • Does not protect against probate challenges if disputes arise among heirs.
  • Not a substitute for Medicaid, MERP, or creditor protection planning.
Pro Tips for Advisors
  • Combine With Other Tools if Needed: If MERP or Medicaid recovery is a concern, consider pairing with a Lady Bird deed or trust.
  • Clarify Ownership Intent: Ensure TODD language is precise to avoid disputes among beneficiaries.
  • Update Periodically: Revise the deed if circumstances or heirs change.
  • Document Everything: Keep copies of recorded deeds and any changes to avoid confusion.

How It Differs from a Lady Bird Deed

A Lady Bird Deed (Enhanced Life Estate Deed) and a TODD serve similar purposes — avoiding probate — but they operate differently and have slightly different purposes. For a better view of how they differ, be sure to read the chapter on Lady Bird Deeds and MERP as it may be more suitable for your end goal purposes.

Real-Life Example

Maria owns her Houston home outright and wants to ensure her two children inherit it without going through probate. She executes a TODD naming both children as beneficiaries. Maria retains full control during her life, including the right to sell or refinance. Upon her death, the property automatically transfers to her children, avoiding the time and expense of probate.

When a TODD Makes Sense

  • You own your home outright or with a spouse and want it to go directly to your kids.
  • You want to avoid probate but don’t need Medicaid asset protection.
  • You don’t have complicated family or business ownership structures.
  • You want to keep control while alive — you can revoke or change beneficiaries anytime.

When a TODD May Not Be Ideal

  • You’re concerned about Medicaid look-back or MERP recovery — use a Lady Bird Deed or Trust instead.
  • You expect beneficiaries to disagree or need shared ownership terms — use a Trust or Partition Agreement.
  • You have multiple properties or plan to change title frequently.
  • You live in a state that does not recognize TODDs (though over half the U.S. now does).

How to Set It Up (Step-by-Step)

  • Obtain the statutory TODD form from your state’s real estate or law library website.
  • List your legal name(s) exactly as shown on your current deed.
  • Identify the beneficiary(ies) — name, address, and relationship.
  • Include the property’s legal description (from your current deed).
  • Sign in front of a notary.
  • Record it with your county clerk’s office — this step is critical for validity.

Pro Tip: The TODD doesn’t transfer ownership until death. That means no gift taxes, no due-on-sale clause, and no impact on your mortgage or homestead exemption while you’re alive.

Potential Tax Implications

When the property transfers after your death, the beneficiary receives a step-up in basis — meaning the property’s tax basis resets to the market value at the date of death. This often eliminates most, if not all, capital gains tax if they sell soon after.

So in essence, a TODD offers:

  • Probate avoidance
  • Tax relief for heirs
  • Retained lifetime control

That’s a triple win for most homeowners.

Real-Life Example

Let’s say Carla, a widow in Houston, owns her home free and clear. She wants her daughter to inherit it but doesn’t want to pay for a trust or drag her through probate.

She records a TODD at the county clerk’s office, naming her daughter as beneficiary.

When Carla passes, her daughter files the death certificate with the county, and the home automatically transfers into her name within days — no court, no waiting, no lawyer bills.

Key Takeaways

  • TODDs are simple, effective tools for passing property to heirs without probate.
  • They offer revocable, controlled transfers during the owner’s lifetime.
  • Best used for straightforward estates where creditor and Medicaid issues are not primary concerns.
  • Advisory note: Always check state-specific requirements and consider complementary estate planning tools for broader protection.

This chapter equips homeowners with a practical, low-friction estate tool — perfect for clients seeking simplicity, clarity, and efficient property transfer.

Step-Up in Basis: How Your Heirs Avoid Capital Gains Taxes on Inherited Property

One of the most valuable and least understood tax advantages in real estate. Includes a scenario comparison table showing exactly who benefits and how much tax is saved.

Many homeowners panic when they think about passing property to their children. “Will they owe decades of capital gains taxes?” they ask. The good news: in most cases, they won’t — thanks to the step-up in basis.

This is one of the least understood, most powerful wealth-building tools in estate planning and real estate investing.

It’s also one of the most underutilized and misunderstood tools in estate planning. For heirs, it can eliminate decades of capital gains taxes, making inherited property far easier and less costly to manage or sell. Understanding this concept is essential for homeowners, investors, and estate planners alike.

What Is Step-Up in Basis?

When a property is inherited, the tax basis “steps up” to its fair market value at the date of the owner’s death.

  • Original owner purchased home: $150,000
  • Value at inheritance: $500,000
  • Step-up in basis: Heirs’ cost basis becomes $500,000, not $150,000
  • Result: If heirs sell immediately, capital gains taxes may be eliminated.

Why It Works

  • Tax Efficiency: Eliminates decades of appreciation from capital gains calculations.
  • Liquidity Preservation: Reduces or removes the tax burden, leaving heirs more cash for investment or reinvestment.
  • Estate Planning Advantage: Works seamlessly with trusts, TODDs, or Lady Bird deeds to streamline inheritance.

Who Benefits Most

ScenarioBenefit
Children inheriting a long-held family home Avoid paying capital gains taxes on decades of appreciation
Spouses Unlimited marital deduction usually applies, plus step-up basis
Heirs inheriting investment propertiesSimplifies the sale process without a hefty tax hit
Estate plannersReduces client concern about tax burden on heirs
Seniors managing their legacyMaximizes wealth transferred without costly tax consequences
Investors inheriting rental properties Step-up can reset depreciation schedule, offering tax planning flexibility
Multi-property estates Each property steps up separately, which can greatly reduce total estate tax exposure

Why Most People Panic Unnecessarily:

Without knowing this rule, heirs often assume they’ll owe capital gains taxes on decades of appreciation — which can create fear and even lead to hasty sales.

The reality: step-up in basis is automatic, as long as the transfer occurs due to death. Beneficiaries rarely need to do anything beyond filing the death certificate and updating their records.

Conditions and Requirements

  • Applies only upon death; does not benefit property transfers during life.
  • Basis adjustment is fair market value at date of death, or alternate valuation date if elected for estate purposes.
  • Applies to property included in the estate, so proper estate planning is critical.
  • Step-up does not prevent other estate taxes, so larger estates may still have obligations.

Timing and Considerations

  • To reiterate, the step-up occurs at the date of death. So it’s always a good idea to have an appraisal of the property done immediately after the inheritance takes place so you have an accurate view of the new step-up in value.
  • If the heir holds the property long-term after inheritance, future appreciation is taxed from the stepped-up basis, not the original purchase price.
  • For couples with trusts, it’s critical to coordinate date-of-death reporting to maximize basis adjustments.
  • Inherited property can be used for gifting or 1031 exchanges, with step-up basis reducing capital gains exposure.
Pro Tips for Advisors
  • Combine With Estate Planning Tools: Step-up works best alongside TODDs, trusts, or Lady Bird deeds.
  • Document Valuation: Ensure proper appraisal at the time of death for accurate basis.
  • Communicate With Heirs: Educate beneficiaries on their new tax basis and potential options.
  • Coordinate With Other Strategies: Consider how capital gains, estate taxes, and Medicaid recovery rules interact with the step-up.
  • Document improvements separately: Step-up applies to the market value, not upgrades — keep receipts for future tax clarity.

Real-Life Example

John inherited his parents’ Houston home, originally purchased for $200,000 and now worth $750,000. Because of the step-up in basis, John’s taxable capital gain is $0 if he sells immediately. Without this step-up, he could have owed taxes on $550,000 of appreciation — potentially hundreds of thousands of dollars — a massive and unnecessary burden.

Key Takeaways

  • Step-up in basis is a powerful, automatic, and often overlooked tool that can eliminate capital gains taxes for heirs.
  • Works best when integrated with estate planning strategies like TODDs, trusts, and Lady Bird deeds. Pairing step-up with TODDs, Lady Bird Deeds, or trusts creates a seamless inheritance path, avoids probate, and minimizes tax impact.
  • Advisory note: Always document valuations and coordinate with a tax professional to maximize benefits. Always coordinate with a qualified CPA or estate attorney to document property value and ensure heirs claim the step-up correctly.

Community Property with Right of Survivorship (CPWROS): The Texas Spousal Transfer Tool

A powerful Texas-specific designation that transfers property to the surviving spouse instantly upon death — no probate, no court, no waiting. With Who Benefits Most table.

For married couples in Texas (and many other states too - check your own local state laws), one of the most overlooked estate planning tools is the Community Property with Right of Survivorship (CPWROS) deed. This simple yet powerful strategy allows spouses to transfer property directly to the surviving spouse, bypassing probate and simplifying the inheritance process — all while preserving tax advantages.

What Is CPWROS?

CPWROS is a deed type that combines the benefits of community property with right of survivorship:

  • Community Property: Assets acquired during marriage are jointly owned.
  • Right of Survivorship: Upon the death of one spouse, ownership automatically transfers to the surviving spouse without probate.

This makes CPWROS a streamlined solution for estate planning, especially for primary residences.

Why It Works

  • Avoids Probate: The property does not go through court, saving time, legal fees, and administrative headaches.
  • Maintains Step-Up in Basis: The surviving spouse receives a stepped-up basis on the property, minimizing potential capital gains taxes if sold later.
  • Preserves Spousal Rights: Ensures the surviving spouse retains full control over the property.
  • Flexible for Planning: Can work alongside trusts or TODDs if additional layers of protection are needed.

Key Considerations

  • Title Must Be Correct: Only properties titled as community property are eligible.
  • Not Suitable for All Assets: Separate property (inherited or pre-marriage assets) may need a different approach.

The property bypasses probate, she can continue living in the home without interruption, and the tax basis for potential sale later is stepped up to the current market value.

Key Takeaways

  • CPWROS is a powerful, low-maintenance estate planning tool for married couples in Texas.
  • It avoids probate, preserves step-up in basis, and ensures spousal ownership continuity.
  • Works best as part of a comprehensive estate plan, alongside trusts, TODDs, or Lady Bird Deeds.
  • Advisory note: Always confirm eligibility and proper deed execution with a qualified real estate attorney.

Partition Agreements: Preventing Family Conflicts After Inheritance

When multiple heirs inherit property together, disagreements can fracture families and destroy value. Includes a full scenario benefits table and conditions checklist.

Inheriting property can be a blessing — until the siblings start disagreeing. Then things can turn messy quickly, especially when multiple heirs receive property. Without a clear plan, disagreements often escalate, leading to court battles, forced sales, or damaged family relationships. A partition agreement is a powerful tool that allows heirs to formally legally binding plan to agree on property use, sale, or buyout terms in advance, preventing disputes and protecting both assets and relationships.

What Is a Partition Agreement?

Strategy Breakdown: How to Set Up Seamless Transfers

When it comes to estate planning, many people assume that having a will is enough. Unfortunately, a will alone doesn’t skip probate — it just tells the court how to divide your estate after that expensive process.

POD (Payable on Death) and TOD (Transfer on Death) designations, on the other hand, cut through all of that. They’re like pre-authorized handshakes between your bank, your investment firms, and your beneficiaries.

Here’s how they work:

  • POD Accounts are typically used for checking, savings, and money market accounts. You list one or more beneficiaries directly with your bank. When you pass, the funds are released to them immediately, outside the court system.
  • TOD Accounts work the same way for investment accounts, such as brokerage or mutual fund accounts. Upon your passing, ownership automatically transfers to your named beneficiaries — without lawyers, delays, or additional fees.

These designations can be added to nearly any financial account — banks, credit unions, brokerage firms, even some CDs and retirement accounts. All it takes is filling out a short form with your institution.

Smart Structuring Tips

  • Use full legal names for your beneficiaries — not nicknames. Typos and incomplete names can cause avoidable disputes.
  • Keep copies of all your designations and update them after major life events — marriage, divorce, births, or deaths.
  • Name contingent beneficiaries (backups) in case your primary beneficiary predeceases you.
  • Coordinate with your estate plan so your POD/TOD designations don’t conflict with your will or trust instructions.
  • Check for account type limits. Some states or institutions have restrictions on which accounts can use TOD or POD.

The beauty of these tools lies in their simplicity. No attorney or court needs to intervene — and no one has to wait months to access critical funds for funeral costs, living expenses, or business continuity.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

Even simple estate tools can backfire if you misunderstand how they interact with other parts of your financial structure. PODs and TODs are powerful, but they’re not foolproof — and once activated (at your death), they can’t be undone.

Here are some of the most common pitfalls to avoid:

  • Thinking joint ownership is “good enough.”

Many people assume that adding a spouse or child as a joint owner on a bank account will achieve the same purpose as a POD. It doesn’t.

Joint ownership gives the other person immediate legal access — meaning they can withdraw or use funds anytime, not just after your passing. Worse, it exposes your account to their creditors, divorces, or lawsuits. A POD or TOD gives control only when you’re gone, not before.

  • Forgetting to update beneficiaries.

Life changes, but too often, paperwork doesn’t. Old designations from decades ago can accidentally leave assets to ex-spouses or deceased relatives. Set a reminder to review all beneficiary designations annually — just like you would your insurance policies or investment portfolio.

  • Assuming a will overrides your POD or TOD.

It doesn’t. These designations take precedence over your will. If your will and account designations don’t match, the financial institution will follow what’s on file for the account — not what your will says. Coordination is key.

  • Ignoring tax implications.

While POD and TOD transfers usually avoid probate, they don’t necessarily avoid taxes. The recipient may still owe taxes on inherited accounts, particularly investment assets or retirement funds. Consulting a CPA or estate attorney can help ensure your loved ones aren’t surprised later.

  • Forgetting about non-transferable assets.

Some assets — like real estate or business interests — require additional planning, such as transfer-on-death deeds or trusts. PODs and TODs don’t apply to everything, so they’re just one part of a full estate blueprint.

  • Neglecting documentation.

Even though the process is simple, keep written proof of every POD and TOD form in your personal files, along with the date and the name of the financial institution representative who processed it. Redundancy prevents confusion.

What Is the Estate Tax?

The estate tax is a federal tax on the total value of everything you own at death — your home(s), investments, cash, vehicles, life insurance, businesses, and personal property — before it’s passed on to heirs.

Now, here’s the good news: most estates don’t owe this tax. That’s because there’s an exemption threshold — meaning your estate isn’t taxed unless it exceeds a certain amount.

As of 2025, the federal estate tax exemption is $13.61 million per person (or $27.22 million for married couples), though this is scheduled to sunset in 2026 and potentially cut in half to around $6–7 million per person, depending on future legislation.

That might still sound like “rich people problems,” but consider this:

  • Real estate values have soared nationwide.
  • Business owners, investors, or retirees with strong portfolios can easily approach the threshold.
  • If Congress lowers the exemption further, millions of middle- to upper-middle-class Americans could find themselves unexpectedly subject to estate taxes.

How the Estate Tax Works

Here’s a simplified example:

Let’s say your estate is worth $15 million.

  • You pass away in 2025.
  • You’re single, so your exemption is $13.61 million.
  • The remaining $1.39 million is taxable at a federal rate of up to 40%.

That’s over $550,000 gone to taxes — not your heirs, not your favorite charity, not your church — but straight to the IRS.

How to Legally and Strategically Avoid the Estate Tax

The key word here is strategically. The IRS isn’t the enemy — but they are the ultimate opportunist if you don’t have a plan.

Here are proven, completely legal ways to minimize or eliminate estate tax exposure:

  • Use the Annual Gift Tax Exclusion

You can give up to $18,000 per person per year (as of 2025) to as many people as you like — completely tax-free.

  • Married couples can double that to $36,000 per person per year.
  • These gifts reduce your taxable estate while helping loved ones now.

Think of it as “pre-gifting your inheritance” — and letting your family (or favorite causes) enjoy your generosity in real time.

  • Establish a Trust

There are several types of trusts that can help remove assets from your taxable estate while maintaining control and protecting your beneficiaries:

  • Revocable Living Trust: Avoids probate, though it doesn’t reduce estate taxes.
  • Irrevocable Trust: Transfers ownership of assets out of your estate, reducing its taxable value.
  • Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT): Lets you donate assets, receive income for life, and leave the remainder to charity — all while lowering estate taxes.

A well-crafted trust can turn a potential tax problem into a wealth-preservation strategy.

  • Use Portability Wisely (for Married Couples)

If you’re married, you and your spouse can combine your exemptions. But to do so, your executor must file an estate tax return (Form 706) after the first spouse’s death — even if no tax is owed.

Failing to do that could forfeit the surviving spouse’s right to “portability” and double the future tax burden.

  • Charitable Giving: Do Good, Pay Less

Strategic philanthropy can reduce your taxable estate and make an impact:

  • Donate appreciated assets to avoid capital gains and shrink your estate.
  • Name charities as beneficiaries in your will or trust.
  • Create a Donor-Advised Fund to give over time with flexibility.

Remember: you can either give your wealth to the IRS or give it purpose.

  • Life Insurance for Estate Liquidity

High-value estates can face “asset-rich, cash-poor” issues — meaning heirs may have to sell property quickly to pay estate taxes.

A life insurance trust (ILIT) can provide immediate, tax-free liquidity to cover estate expenses and protect your family’s holdings from being liquidated under pressure.

Insider Tip: Start Early

The most effective estate tax strategies require foresight. Once you pass, your ability to move, restructure, or protect assets ends — and your executor’s hands are tied.

Meet with a trusted estate attorney or financial planner now to:

  • Evaluate your net worth and potential exposure
  • Update wills, trusts, and beneficiary designations
  • Document charitable intentions and gifting plans

The sooner you plan, the more options you have.

Real-Life Example

A Houston couple owned multiple properties, a thriving small business, and investment accounts totaling $12 million. At first glance, they assumed they were below the estate tax threshold. But after business growth and real estate appreciation, their estate ballooned to $15.5 million — nearly $2 million above the exemption.

By transferring a vacation home into an irrevocable trust, gifting annual amounts to their children, and creating a charitable remainder trust, they reduced their taxable estate by over $3 million — effectively eliminating their estate tax liability entirely.

Insider Takeaway

The estate tax isn’t just about money — it’s about control. Without a plan, you’re letting Washington decide where your life’s work goes.

But with a bit of proactive strategy, you can ensure your legacy passes to your family, your community, or your causes — not the IRS.

Because after all, you already paid taxes to build your life. You shouldn’t have to pay them again to leave it behind.

CDPAP: Turning Family Caregiving Into a Wealth-Building Strategy

Consumer Directed Personal Assistance Programs allow family members to be compensated for caregiving — turning an unpaid burden into a legitimate income stream while preserving assets.

Most families eventually face one of the hardest financial challenges of their lives: caring for an aging parent, chronically ill spouse, or disabled child. Long-term care is expensive, draining, and emotionally heavy. Without planning, it can quietly evaporate decades of savings, equity, and inheritance — not because of irresponsibility, but because the system is structured to bankrupt the unprepared.

But in New York (and some other states with similar programs), families have access to a powerful, often misunderstood tool that can change the entire economic trajectory of long-term care:

CDPAP — the Consumer Directed Personal Assistance Program.

CDPAP is more than a caregiving model.

It is a financial shield, a family-support mechanism, and a wealth-preservation tool disguised as healthcare assistance.

This chapter unpacks how CDPAP works, how to qualify, how to integrate it into your overall wealth architecture, and how to use it to preserve dignity, protect assets, and support family caregivers without collapsing under financial strain.

  • What CDPAP Actually Is — In Wealth Architect Terms

CDPAP allows Medicaid-eligible individuals to hire family members, friends, or trusted individuals to provide care — and the caregiver gets paid.

Unlike traditional home health aides, CDPAP allows you to choose someone who:

  • Loves you
  • Actually shows up
  • Understands your needs
  • Speaks your language
  • Honors your dignity

And the caregiver can be:

  • A child
  • A sibling
  • A niece/nephew
  • A long-term friend
  • A trusted church member

The only typical exclusions:

spouses and legally responsible parents of minors cannot be paid (but adult children can be).

This reverses the usual burden:

Instead of draining family resources, the state pays your family to care for you.

  • How CDPAP Protects Family Wealth

Most families lose wealth during elder care in two primary ways:

  • Paying out-of-pocket for caregivers
  • Quitting jobs or reducing work hours to care for loved ones

CDPAP neutralizes both.

Financial Advantages:

A. Paid Caregiving Keeps Income Inside the Family Unit

Instead of paying a stranger $20–$30/hr, your chosen caregiver gets paid through Medicaid.

That income:

  • Supports the caregiver financially
  • Helps compensate for lost working hours
  • Keeps money circulating within the family’s economic ecosystem

This is not “side income.”

This is structural income redirection.

B. Delays or Prevents Nursing Home Placement

Nursing homes can cost $8,000–$15,000 per month.

CDPAP keeps the loved one at home longer, avoiding:

  • Liquidation of investments
  • Forced sale of the home
  • Medicaid “spend-down” wipeouts
  • Emotional trauma

Every month a family member stays out of a nursing home because of CDPAP is a month of preserved wealth.

C. Enables Strategic Medicaid Planning Without Guilt

For families working with an elder law attorney to qualify for Medicaid:

  • CDPAP makes home care affordable and sustainable, covering what would otherwise require impossible costs.
  • It also fits naturally into Medicaid asset protection trusts, gifting strategies, and look-back planning.

CDPAP becomes part of the architecture that protects:

  • Home equity
  • IRAs
  • Savings
  • Life insurance cash value
  • Inherited assets
  • The family business
  • Integrating CDPAP Into Long-Term Wealth Preservation

This is where most families miss the mark.

CDPAP isn’t just a caregiving program — it’s a piece of a larger strategy.

Here’s how it fits into your wealth blueprint:

A. Combine CDPAP With a Medicaid Asset Protection Trust (MAPT)

When structured correctly:

  • The home is placed in a trust
  • Savings and investments are protected
  • Medicaid handles the care
  • CDPAP pays a trusted caregiver

The state can’t touch the trust assets after the look-back period.

The family keeps the estate intact.

This is one of the most powerful wealth-protection moves a family can make.

B. Use CDPAP to Buy Time for Proper Estate Planning

Families often panic and make rushed decisions.

CDPAP stabilizes the situation so you can:

  • Establish health proxies
  • Update wills
  • Create living wills
  • Protect property titles
  • Consolidate accounts
  • Assign POD/TOD beneficiaries
  • Secure DNR or advanced directives

CDPAP gives breathing room to set the legal architecture correctly.

C. Use CDPAP as Income Replacement for a Family Caregiver

When an adult child must stop working to care for a parent, CDPAP compensates them.

This preserves:

  • Their credit
  • Their retirement contributions
  • Their ability to pay rent/mortgage
  • Their financial independence

Without CDPAP, caregivers often fall into poverty while trying to keep loved ones out of nursing homes.

This program prevents generational financial collapse in caregiving families.

**4. Who Qualifies for CDPAP?

Understanding Eligibility in Simple Terms**

Eligibility is straightforward:

  • The person receiving care must be Medicaid-eligible
  • Income limits vary by county
  • Many families qualify after Medicaid planning
  • They must require assistance with daily activities, such as:
  • Bathing
  • Dressing
  • Eating
  • Mobility
  • Medication reminders
  • Chronic condition management
  • They must be capable of directing their care, or have a designated representative.

That’s it.

No medical degree.

No home health agency telling you who can help.

No strangers entering your home unless you choose them.

  • How to Start CDPAP — A Quick Blueprint

Step 1: Determine Medicaid Eligibility

If the patient is not yet eligible, meet with an elder law attorney or Medicaid planner to structure assets.

Step 2: Get a Doctor’s Order

The physician submits a form stating the patient needs home care services.

Step 3: CDPAP Evaluation

A nurse assessor determines how many weekly care hours the patient qualifies for.

Step 4: Choose the Caregiver

The family member applies, completes onboarding, and is hired through a fiscal intermediary (FI).

Step 5: Caregiver Begins Paid Caregiving

Payroll, training, and timekeeping go through the FI — not the patient.

CDPAP Nationwide: State-by-State Programs That Let Families Get Paid to Care for Loved Ones

While New York’s CDPAP is the most famous, every U.S. state has some form of self-directed, consumer-directed, or participant-directed home care through Medicaid. The names differ, the eligibility varies, and some states restrict who can be paid — but the underlying principle remains: a senior or disabled individual can choose their caregiver, and in most states, that caregiver can be a family member.

Below is the full list of each state and its CDPAP-like program.

Alabama — Personal Choices Program

Alabama’s Personal Choices Program is its self-directed Medicaid option. Participants manage their own care budget and can hire family members, excluding spouses and legal guardians in most cases.

Alaska — Personal Care Services (PCS) & Community First Choice (CFC)

Alaska allows self-direction under both PCS and CFC. Family caregivers are permitted, though spouses are evaluated case-by-case.

Arizona — Self-Directed Attendant Care (SDAC)

Arizona’s long-running SDAC model allows beneficiaries to hire family caregivers, but spouses and parents of minors may face limitations.

Arkansas — ARChoices in Homecare

This program offers a self-directed option that allows hiring family members other than spouses. Parents may be paid caregivers if the child is 18 or older.

California — In-Home Supportive Services (IHSS)

California’s IHSS is one of the broadest systems in the country. Most family members — including adult children, relatives, and even spouses in certain circumstances — can be paid.

Colorado — Consumer-Directed Attendant Support Services (CDASS)

CDASS under Colorado’s HCBS waivers allows participants to hire nearly any qualified adult, including family, but typically excludes spouses and legal guardians.

Connecticut — Personal Care Assistance (PCA) Waiver

Connecticut’s PCA waiver allows self-direction and permits most family caregivers except spouses or legally responsible relatives for minors.

Delaware — Personal Attendant Services (PAS)

Delaware’s PAS program allows self-direction and caregiver choice, with family caregiver payments evaluated individually.

Florida — Consumer-Directed Care Plus (CDC+)

Florida’s CDC+ program gives beneficiaries budget authority and allows them to hire family members other than spouses or guardians.

Georgia — Independent Care Waiver Program (ICWP)

Georgia permits self-direction under the ICWP and SOURCE waivers, allowing family caregivers with standard exclusions.

Hawaii — Community Choices & Kupuna Caregiver Support

Hawaii’s Medicaid self-direction allows family caregivers; the state also offers non-Medicaid caregiver pay through Kupuna Care.

Idaho — Self-Direction Option

Idaho allows hiring family caregivers for personal care, excluding spouses in some cases.

Illinois — Home Services Program (HSP)

Illinois’ HSP allows hiring family members, including adult children, but not spouses.

Indiana — Aged & Disabled Waiver Self-Directed Attendant Care

Indiana allows self-direction and paying family caregivers, but not spouses or legal guardians.

Iowa — Consumer Choices Option (CCO)

Iowa’s CCO provides budget authority permitting payment to family caregivers except spouses.

Kansas — Self-Directed Personal Care Services

Kansas allows hiring most family members but generally excludes spouses and parents of minors.

Kentucky — Participant-Directed Services (PDS)

Kentucky’s PDS waiver allows hiring almost all family members except spouses.

Louisiana — Community Choices Waiver (CCW) & LTPCS

Louisiana allows participant direction under multiple waivers, and family caregivers can be hired with standard exclusions.

Maine — Section 19 & Section 96 Self-Direction

Maine allows hiring most family members, excluding spouses and guardians.

Maryland — Community First Choice (CFC) Self-Directed Services

Maryland allows family caregivers, though spouses and parents of minors are typically not allowed.

Massachusetts — Personal Care Attendant (PCA) Program

Massachusetts permits hiring family caregivers except for spouses.

Michigan — MI Choice Waiver (Self-Determination)

Michigan’s self-directed option allows hiring family members, excluding spouses and guardians.

Minnesota — Consumer Directed Community Supports (CDCS)

Minnesota has broad self-direction flexibility; many family members, including parents of adult children, may be paid caregivers.

Mississippi — Independent Living Waiver Self-Direction

Mississippi allows hiring family caregivers other than spouses or parents of minors.

Missouri — Consumer-Directed Services (CDS)

Missouri’s CDS program allows hiring almost any family member, excluding spouses.

Montana — Self-Directed Personal Assistance Services

Montana permits family caregivers, with case-by-case review for spouses.

Nebraska — Aged & Disabled Waiver Self-Directed Option

Nebraska allows beneficiaries to hire family caregivers except spouses.

Nevada — Personal Care Services (PCS) Self-Directed

Nevada allows hiring family caregivers, but spouses and parents of minors are limited.

New Hampshire — Participant-Directed Services

Most family caregivers are allowed; spouses are restricted.

New Jersey — Personal Preference Program (PPP)

New Jersey’s PPP allows hiring almost any family member except spouses or guardians.

New Mexico — Self-Directed Community Benefit

New Mexico has one of the most flexible programs; family caregivers are widely permitted.

New York — CDPAP

New York has the most flexible and generous program: spouses, adult children, family members, and even close friends can be paid caregivers.

North Carolina — Community Alternatives Program (CAP) Self-Directed Option

North Carolina allows family caregivers but excludes spouses and parents of minors.

North Dakota — Community Support Services Self-Directed Care

Family caregivers are allowed with standard limitations.

Ohio — Ohio Home Care Waiver Self-Directed Option

Ohio allows hiring family caregivers except spouses.

Oklahoma — Self-Directed Personal Care Services

Oklahoma allows hiring most family members except spouses.

Oregon — OR Self-Directed Services (Brokerage Model)

Oregon allows family caregivers broadly, including some spousal cases.

Pennsylvania — Services My Way (FMS Model)

Pennsylvania allows hiring family caregivers, though spouses may be restricted depending on waiver.

Rhode Island — Personal Choice Program

Rhode Island allows hiring family caregivers except spouses.

South Carolina — Community Choices Waiver Self-Directed Option

South Carolina permits family caregivers but excludes spouses.

South Dakota — Personal Attendant Services Self-Directed

South Dakota allows hiring most family members with spousal restrictions.

Tennessee — Consumer Direction (CHoICES & ECFC)

Tennessee allows hiring family caregivers but excludes spouses.

Texas — Consumer Directed Services (CDS)

Texas allows hiring many family caregivers, though spouses are generally excluded.

Utah — Self-Administered Services (SAS)

Utah allows most family caregivers except spouses and parents of minors.

Vermont — Flexible Choices

Vermont allows broad caregiver choice, though spouses may be restricted.

Virginia — Consumer-Directed Services (CD) under multiple waivers

Virginia allows hiring family caregivers, excluding spouses and parents of minors.

Washington — Consumer-Directed Employer (CDE) Program

Washington allows payment to family caregivers, including some spouses with specific approvals.

West Virginia — Aged & Disabled Waiver Self-Directed Option

West Virginia allows most family caregivers except spouses.

Wisconsin — IRIS (Include, Respect, I Self-Direct)

Wisconsin has a robust self-directed model allowing many family caregivers but limiting spouses.

Wyoming — Community Choices Waiver Self-Direction

Wyoming permits hiring family caregivers with standard limitations.
